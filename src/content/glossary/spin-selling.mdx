definition: "SPIN selling is a discovery-call framework based on four question types: Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff. Developed by Neil Rackham in 1988, it's still taught at most enterprise sales training programs today."
longExplanation: |
  Most candidates can recite SPIN's four question types. The hiring signal is whether you've actually used the sequence on a deal. Situation questions establish context; Problem questions surface pain; Implication questions force the prospect to articulate cost of inaction; Need-payoff questions get the prospect to sell themselves on the solution.
  
  SPIN is heavier than BANT and lighter than MEDDPICC. It overlaps with discovery best practices and pairs naturally with MEDDPICC's Identified Pain field. Hiring managers at older enterprise SaaS shops (the ones that grew up on Rackham's research) often probe SPIN explicitly; newer-cohort hiring managers tend to use SPIN principles without the brand name.
workedExample: |
  Prospect runs a logistics company; their dispatchers manage routes manually.
  
  Situation: "Walk me through how your dispatch team builds routes today."
  
  Problem: "Where do you see the most rework or delays in that process?"
  
  Implication: "When dispatch loses 30 minutes rebuilding a route, what does that cost you in driver utilization or customer SLA?"
  
  Need-payoff: "If you could give your dispatchers a tool that cut that rework time by half, how would that change your weekly driver-hour budget?"
  
  The prospect now owns the value case. SPIN works because the prospect closes themselves; you don't have to pitch.

# SPIN selling

SPIN selling is a discovery-call framework based on four question types: Situation, Problem, Implication, Need-payoff. Developed by Neil Rackham in 1988, it's still taught at most enterprise sales training programs today.
