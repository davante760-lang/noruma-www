longExplanation: |
  Behavioral questions dominate the hiring-manager and panel rounds in sales interviews. STAR forces you to start with context (what the situation was), your responsibility (what you owned), what you actually did (the action), and the measurable outcome (the result). Without a quantified result, STAR is just a story.
  
  Most candidates fail STAR by spending 80% of the answer on Situation and Task. The hiring signal is in Action and Result. Trim setup; expand on what you did and what number it moved.
workedExample: |
  Question: "Tell me about a deal you lost and what you'd do differently."
  
  Bad answer: "We were selling to a large enterprise customer in a competitive RFP situation. The decision-making process was complex and there were many stakeholders involved..." (90 seconds of Situation, no Action, no Result.)
  
  Strong answer: "Acme Corp, $400k ARR, lost in week 6 of the cycle. (S) Procurement-driven RFP with three vendors. (T) I was lead AE on a year-long land-and-expand. (A) I spent rounds 1-3 building a champion in IT but didn't multi-thread to finance until round 4 when our champion went on PTO and went dark. (R) Lost to a competitor on price, $400k ARR. Lesson: in any procurement-led deal, finance gets a stakeholder slot in week 1, not week 4. I now multi-thread mandatory above $200k ACV."

# STAR method

STAR is a behavioral-question framework: Situation, Task, Action, Result. It structures answers to questions like "tell me about a time you lost a deal" into a four-part narrative.
