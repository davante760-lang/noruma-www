You're in the final round for an AE role. The hiring manager leans forward: *"Tell me about a time when you turned around a struggling deal."*

Your mind races through a dozen half-remembered deals. Which one shows the right skills? How much detail is too much? What if you blank on the numbers?

This question tests three things at once: your ability to diagnose problems, your persistence under pressure, and your storytelling skills. Hiring managers want to see that you don't give up when deals stall—and that you can identify the root cause instead of just throwing solutions at the wall.

Here's how to answer it in a way that lands the offer.

## What Hiring Managers Are Really Asking

When interviewers ask about turning around struggling deals, they're evaluating:

**Problem diagnosis:** Can you identify why a deal is stalling? Is it budget, timing, stakeholder alignment, competition, or something else?

**Strategic thinking:** Do you have a playbook for recovering deals, or do you just hope for the best?

**Persistence vs. desperation:** Did you push intelligently, or did you annoy the buyer with daily check-ins?

**Outcome orientation:** Did you actually close it, or did you just reschedule a few times before it died?

The best answers show pattern recognition—you've been in this situation before, you know the warning signs, and you have a repeatable process for getting deals back on track.

## The STAR Framework for Turnaround Stories

Use STAR (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to structure your answer. This keeps you focused and prevents you from rambling through unnecessary details.

### Situation: Set up the deal context

Start with the basics: company size, deal value, timeline, and why it mattered. Then describe the stall.

**Good setup:**
*"I was working a $180K annual deal with a Series B fintech company—about 220 employees. We'd been in conversations for six weeks, had three strong discovery calls with the VP of Sales and her team, and sent a proposal. Then radio silence. No responses to emails, no follow-ups scheduled. The deal was marked 'closed-lost' in our CRM after two weeks of no contact."*

**Why this works:** You've quantified the deal, established timeline, and described the problem clearly. The interviewer knows exactly what "struggling" means in this context.

### Task: Clarify your role

Make it clear this was your deal to save, not something your manager stepped in to rescue.

**Good task statement:**
*"My goal was to figure out why we went dark and whether the deal was truly dead—or if there was a way to re-engage without being pushy."*

**Why this works:** Shows ownership and a thoughtful approach. You're not just blindly following up.

### Action: Walk through your recovery strategy

This is where you demonstrate your diagnostic skills and recovery playbook. Break it into steps:

#### Step 1: Diagnose the stall

> "First, I checked LinkedIn. The VP of Sales had posted about their Q4 priorities two days earlier—revenue operations was front and center, but there was no mention of the problem our product solved. That told me we'd either lost to a competitor or been deprioritized."

#### Step 2: Find a different entry point

> "Instead of emailing the VP again, I reached out to the Head of RevOps—someone I'd connected with during our second call but hadn't directly engaged with since. I sent a short message acknowledging that timing might have changed, and asked if they'd be open to a quick call to share what we'd learned from similar companies in their space."

#### Step 3: Re-establish value with new information

> "On that call, I learned their CFO had put a temporary freeze on new tools while they evaluated their entire tech stack. The VP hadn't ghosted us—she just didn't want to waste our time. I asked if I could send over a lightweight one-pager showing how we'd helped two other fintech companies consolidate tools instead of adding another one. She said yes."

#### Step 4: Create a new path forward

> "I worked with our solutions engineer to build a custom ROI model showing how our platform could replace two tools they were already using, cutting their total spend by $40K annually while solving the original problem. The Head of RevOps loved it, brought it to the CFO, and we were back in active discussions within a week."

**Why this works:**

- Shows a logical diagnostic process (LinkedIn research)
- Demonstrates relationship skills (finding a new champion)
- Proves strategic thinking (reframing value around their new constraint)
- Quantifies impact ($40K savings)

### Result: Close with numbers and lessons

End with the outcome and what you learned that you've applied since.

**Good result:**
*"We closed the deal six weeks later at $165K—slightly smaller scope than the original proposal, but higher margin because we positioned as a consolidation play. More importantly, I learned that 'no response' doesn't always mean 'no interest.' Sometimes buyers go dark because their priorities shifted, not because you did something wrong. Now, when a deal stalls, I do LinkedIn and news research before I assume it's dead, and I look for alternative entry points instead of just emailing the same person."*

**Why this works:**

- Closed-won outcome with dollar value
- Honest about scope change (builds credibility)
- Extracted a repeatable lesson
- Shows pattern recognition for future deals

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Choosing a deal that never actually closed

If the deal didn't close, don't use it for this question. "Turned around" means you won the business, not just got another meeting.

**Exception:** If the interviewer specifically asks about deals you *tried* to save, then you can share a loss—but be clear about what you learned and how you've improved since.

### 2. Making yourself the hero without acknowledging help

If your SE, manager, or another team member played a key role, say so. Giving credit makes you more credible.

**Good phrasing:**
*"I worked with our solutions engineer to build a custom ROI model..."*

**Bad phrasing:**
*"I personally built a custom ROI model..."* (unless you actually did)

### 3. Blaming the buyer or your team

Never say the deal stalled because:

- The champion was lazy
- Your marketing team sent bad content
- The buyer "didn't understand the value"

Excuses kill credibility. Take ownership of the diagnosis and recovery.

### 4. Rambling through every email and call

Your interviewer doesn't need a play-by-play. Hit the key decision points:

- What changed that caused the stall
- Your diagnostic process
- The strategic shift that re-engaged them
- The outcome

Skip the filler. Keep it tight.

### 5. Forgetting to quantify

Numbers make stories credible. Include:

- Deal value
- Timeline (how long stalled, how long to close after recovery)
- Savings, ROI, or business impact you created
- Company size or context

Vague stories are forgettable. Specific stories land offers.

## How to Adapt This Answer for Different Roles

### For SDR → AE promotion interviews

Focus on what you did to *diagnose* and *re-engage*, even if the AE closed the deal. Show you understand deal dynamics beyond just booking meetings.

**Adaptation:**
*"I noticed the AE's deal had stalled after the demo. I reached out to the contact who'd originally taken my call, mentioned we had new case studies in their vertical, and asked if anything had changed on their end. Turned out their VP had just returned from leave and needed a recap. I helped the AE re-engage with a fresh exec summary, and we closed it three weeks later."*

### For AE → Enterprise AE interviews

Emphasize multi-threading and executive engagement in your turnaround strategy.

**Adaptation:**
*"When the deal stalled at the director level, I leveraged our executive sponsor program to set up a peer call between our CRO and their VP of Sales. That conversation surfaced a board-level initiative we hadn't known about, and we repositioned our solution around that strategic priority."*

### For Sales Manager interviews

Show how you coached a rep through a turnaround, not just how you saved your own deal.

**Adaptation:**
*"One of my reps had a $240K deal go dark after the CFO pushed back on ROI. I walked her through deal forensics—reviewing the discovery calls, identifying gaps in our business case, and finding a new angle. We brought in customer success to share retention data that de-risked the investment, and she closed it six weeks later. I've since built that diagnostic checklist into our team's stalled-deal playbook."*

## When You Don't Have a Perfect Turnaround Story

If you're early in your career or genuinely don't have a clean turnaround example, here are two options:

### Option 1: Share a partial recovery

Talk about a deal that *didn't* close, but where you applied a smart strategy to re-engage.

**Frame it honestly:**
*"I have a story about a deal I tried to turn around—it ultimately didn't close, but the process taught me a lot about diagnosing stalls. [Tell the story using STAR.] Even though we didn't win that one, I've since applied that diagnostic approach to three other stalled deals, and two of them closed."*

### Option 2: Talk about a deal that *almost* stalled but you caught early

If you've never had a full radio-silence scenario, talk about a deal where you noticed warning signs and course-corrected before it went dark.

**Example:**
*"I don't have a story where a deal was marked 'closed-lost' and I brought it back, but I do have one where I caught a stall before it became a ghost situation. [Tell the story using STAR.] I've learned that the best turnarounds are the ones you prevent by staying close to the buyer and tracking engagement signals early."*

## How to Practice This Answer

### 1. Write out your full story using STAR

Don't just rehearse in your head. Write it down. This forces you to tighten the details and cut unnecessary fluff.

### 2. Time yourself

Aim for 90-120 seconds. If you're going over two minutes, you're including too much detail.

### 3. Record yourself

Use your phone or laptop. Listen back. Are you using filler words? Rambling? Underselling the impact?

### 4. Get feedback from a peer

Ask another sales professional to listen to your answer and tell you:

- Was the problem clear?
- Did the solution make sense?
- Did I quantify enough?
- Was it memorable?

### 5. Prepare 2-3 turnaround stories

Different interviewers care about different aspects:

- One story about re-engaging a ghosted buyer
- One story about overcoming a competitive threat
- One story about navigating budget constraints

Having multiple examples lets you adapt based on the interviewer's follow-up questions.

## What to Do If They Ask Follow-Up Questions

Good interviewers will dig deeper. Be ready for:

### "How did you know the deal was worth saving?"

> "I looked at fit—they had the right pain, budget, and timeline before we stalled. The VP had been engaged in discovery and had forwarded our content internally. That signaled real interest, not just tire-kicking. I also checked intent data—they were still researching our category, which meant the problem hadn't gone away."

### "What would you have done differently?"

> "I would've multi-threaded earlier. If I'd built a relationship with the Head of RevOps from the start, I might have learned about the CFO's freeze before we sent the proposal. Now I make sure I'm connected to at least two stakeholders in every deal, so I have an alternate route if one person goes dark."

### "What did your manager think of your approach?"

> "I updated her before reaching back out to the RevOps lead. She supported the approach but suggested I frame the outreach as 'sharing insights' rather than 'trying to revive a dead deal.' That made the message feel less pushy and more consultative."
