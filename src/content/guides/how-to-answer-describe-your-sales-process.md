You're interviewing for an AE role. The hiring manager leans forward and asks: "Walk me through your sales process."

This question isn't casual small talk. It's a test.

They're checking if you:
1. Have a repeatable process (or just wing it every time)
2. Understand the full sales cycle (not just demos and closes)
3. Can articulate methodology clearly (critical for coaching, forecasting, and scaling)
4. Know what activities drive outcomes

A weak answer sounds like this: "I prospect, qualify, demo, close." That's not a process — it's a to-do list.

A strong answer shows structure, sequencing, milestones, and metrics. Here's how to build one.

## Why Interviewers Ask About Your Sales Process

**What they're really asking:**

- Do you have a system, or do you rely on luck?
- Can you diagnose where deals get stuck?
- Do you understand leading vs. lagging indicators?
- Will you be coachable when we onboard you?

**Red flags they're watching for:**

- Vague answers ("I just build relationships and close deals")
- No mention of qualification or discovery
- Skipping metrics entirely
- Process that doesn't match the role (transactional process for a 6-month enterprise sales cycle)

**Green flags they want to see:**

- Clear stages with exit criteria
- Metrics at each stage (conversion rates, cycle time)
- Flexibility within structure (not robotic)
- Buyer-centric language (not seller-centric: "I do X" vs. "The buyer needs Y")

## The Framework: Stages, Activities, Exit Criteria

Break your process into 5-7 stages. For each stage, describe:

1. **Activities** — What do you do?
2. **Exit criteria** — What qualifies a deal to move forward?
3. **Metrics** — What does good look like?

### Example: Mid-Market SaaS AE Process

**Stage 1: Prospecting**
- **Activities:** Build target account list (50-100 accounts matching ICP), research triggers (funding, hiring, tech stack changes), cold outreach (email sequences, LinkedIn, calls)
- **Exit criteria:** First meeting booked with a qualified contact (director+ level, owns the problem)
- **Metrics:** 20-30 touches per day, 2-3 first meetings booked per week

**Stage 2: Discovery**
- **Activities:** Understand pain (current state, impact, urgency), map stakeholders (who else cares?), confirm budget and timeline, present high-level solution fit
- **Exit criteria:** Pain confirmed, budget confirmed, 2+ stakeholders engaged, next step = demo or deeper dive
- **Metrics:** 60% of discovery calls → demo stage

**Stage 3: Demo / Solution Presentation**
- **Activities:** Tailor demo to their use case, tie features to pain points, handle technical questions (loop in SE if needed), confirm value hypothesis
- **Exit criteria:** Champion says "this solves our problem," technical buyer confirms feasibility, agreement to move to evaluation
- **Metrics:** 50% of demos → proposal stage

**Stage 4: Evaluation / Proposal**
- **Activities:** Send proposal with pricing, build business case (ROI, time-to-value), multithread into finance/legal/IT, handle objections (price, competitors, timing)
- **Exit criteria:** Verbal commitment, procurement engaged, legal review started
- **Metrics:** 70% of proposals → negotiation stage, average 2-3 weeks in this stage

**Stage 5: Negotiation**
- **Activities:** Negotiate terms (discounts, payment terms, MSA edits), navigate security questionnaire, align on implementation timeline
- **Exit criteria:** Contract signed, PO received
- **Metrics:** 80% close rate once in negotiation, 5-7 days average

**Stage 6: Closed-Won Handoff**
- **Activities:** Intro to CSM, kickoff call scheduled, internal win debrief (what worked, what almost killed it)
- **Exit criteria:** Customer onboarded, first value milestone hit
- **Metrics:** 90-day retention rate for new customers

## Tailoring by Role

### If you're an SDR

Focus on **prospecting** and **qualification**.

"My process has three stages: research, outreach, and qualification. I start by building a target list of 100 accounts per quarter that match our ICP — usually Series B SaaS companies with 50-200 employees. I research each account for triggers like recent funding or job postings that signal growth. Then I run multi-channel sequences: email, LinkedIn, and cold calls. My goal is 3-4 first meetings booked per week. In qualification, I use BANT to confirm budget, authority, need, and timeline. If all four check out, I hand the lead to my AE with a summary of the pain points and next steps. My discovery-to-SQL conversion rate is around 65%."

### If you're an Enterprise AE

Focus on **account planning**, **multithreading**, and **executive access**.

"My process is account-based. I start with deep research on 10-15 target accounts per quarter — org charts, tech stack, news, financial filings. I identify 2-3 entry points (VPs of Sales, RevOps, or Sales Ops leaders). First call is always discovery: current state, pain, strategic priorities, and stakeholders. From there, I map the buying committee — typically 4-6 people across Sales, IT, Finance, and Ops. I run separate discovery with each stakeholder, then a tailored demo that addresses their specific pain. After the demo, I build a business case with my champion — usually an ROI model showing cost savings or revenue impact. That gets us to the proposal stage. During evaluation, I'm multithreading constantly — executive sponsor calls, technical deep-dives with IT, procurement alignment with Finance. My close rate is 60% once we're in evaluation, and my average deal size is $250k ACV with a 5-month sales cycle."

### If you're a transactional/velocity AE

Focus on **speed** and **volume**.

"My process is high-velocity. I'm working 40-50 active opportunities at any given time. Most deals come from inbound leads — marketing-qualified demos or free trial users. My first call is a 15-minute discovery: what brought you here, what problem are you solving, who else is involved, what's your timeline? If they qualify (budget over $10k, use case matches, decision in 30 days), I move them to a demo the same week. Demo is 30 minutes, focused on their specific use case. If they're interested, I send a proposal within 24 hours. From there, I'm running tight follow-up — email, calls, video snippets — to handle objections and push toward close. My goal is to close deals in 14-21 days from first call. Conversion rate from demo to close is around 30%, and I close 8-12 deals per month."

## Example Answer (Full Delivery)

**Interviewer:** "Walk me through your sales process."

**You:**

"Sure. My process has six stages: prospecting, discovery, demo, proposal, negotiation, and closed-won handoff. Let me walk through each.

**Prospecting:** I start by building a target account list — usually 50-75 companies that match our ICP, which in my current role is Series B SaaS companies selling to mid-market. I research each account for triggers like recent funding, job postings, or tech stack changes that signal they might have the pain we solve. Then I run multi-channel outreach: cold email sequences, LinkedIn messages, and calls. My goal is to book 2-3 first meetings per week with director-level or above contacts.

**Discovery:** First call is all about understanding their current state and pain. I ask about their process today, what's broken, what's the impact, and who else cares about solving it. I also confirm budget and timeline. If the pain is real, the budget exists, and there are 2+ stakeholders, I move them to a demo. My conversion rate from discovery to demo is around 60%.

**Demo:** I tailor the demo to their specific use case — I never run a generic feature tour. I tie every feature back to the pain points they mentioned in discovery. If there are technical questions, I loop in my SE. At the end, I confirm: does this solve your problem? If yes, we move to evaluation.

**Proposal:** I send a proposal within 48 hours with pricing, ROI justification, and next steps. At this stage, I'm multithreading — talking to IT about security, Finance about budget approval, and the executive sponsor about strategic fit. I'm also handling objections around price, competitors, and timing. About 50% of demos move to this stage.

**Negotiation:** Once we have verbal commitment, I work with procurement and legal to finalize terms. This usually takes 5-7 days — MSA edits, security questionnaire, payment terms. My close rate is around 80% once we're in negotiation.

**Closed-won handoff:** After the deal closes, I intro the customer to their CSM, schedule a kickoff call, and do an internal debrief with my manager on what worked and what almost killed the deal. That feedback loop helps me refine my process for the next quarter.

Overall, my sales cycle is 60-75 days from first call to close, and my win rate is about 40% from demo to closed-won."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. No metrics

Saying "I prospect, demo, and close" without numbers makes you sound junior. Always quantify: conversion rates, cycle time, win rates.

### 2. Seller-centric language

"I do a discovery call, I run a demo, I send a proposal" focuses on *your* activities. Reframe around the buyer: "The buyer confirms pain, the champion builds internal support, the committee evaluates fit."

### 3. One-size-fits-all process

If you're interviewing for an enterprise role, don't describe a transactional process. Tailor your answer to the sales cycle length and complexity of the role.

### 4. No flexibility

Saying "I always do 7 steps in this exact order" sounds robotic. Sales is messy. Acknowledge when you skip steps, collapse stages, or loop back to discovery mid-deal.

### 5. Skipping qualification

If your process jumps from "first call" to "demo" without mention of qualification, you sound like you chase bad deals. Always describe how you decide whether to invest time.

## How to Prepare This Answer

### Step 1: Map your actual process

Write down the stages you use today. Be honest — if you don't have a formal process, acknowledge it and describe what you'd build.

### Step 2: Add metrics

Go through your CRM or deal tracker. What are your real conversion rates? Average cycle time? Win rates by stage?

### Step 3: Check for buyer language

Replace "I do X" with "The buyer needs Y" or "We confirm Z together."

### Step 4: Rehearse out loud

Record yourself answering the question. Does it sound natural? Is it under 2 minutes? If it's too long, trim the details.

### Step 5: Tailor to the role

If the job description mentions "6-month sales cycle," emphasize account planning and multithreading. If it says "high-velocity," emphasize speed and volume.

## Variations of This Question

Interviewers ask this question in different ways. Same answer structure, slightly tweaked framing:

- "How do you manage your pipeline?"
- "Walk me through a typical sales cycle."
- "What's your methodology?"
- "How do you move deals from first call to close?"
- "Describe how you qualify and close opportunities."

## What to Do If You Don't Have a Formal Process Yet

If you're early in your career or switching from a different role, you can still give a strong answer by describing what you'd build:

**Example:**

"I'm early in my sales career, so I haven't had the chance to build out a full process yet. But based on what I've learned from top performers and sales training, here's the process I'd follow: I'd start with prospecting — building a target account list and running multi-channel outreach. Then discovery to confirm pain, budget, and timeline. If they qualify, I'd move to a demo tailored to their use case. After that, a proposal with clear ROI, followed by negotiation and close. I'd track conversion rates at each stage so I can diagnose where deals get stuck and improve. In my first 90 days, I'd refine this process based on what I learn from shadowing calls and deal reviews with my manager."
