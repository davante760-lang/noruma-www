You're in a sales interview. The hiring manager asks: "How do you handle
objections?"

This is one of the most common sales interview questions, and it's a test
of your sales fundamentals.

Weak answers sound generic: "I listen to the customer and address their
concerns." Strong answers show a repeatable framework, give real examples,
and demonstrate that you see objections as opportunities, not roadblocks.

Here's how to answer the objection handling question in a way that proves
you're a closer, not just a talker.

## Why Interviewers Ask About Objection Handling

**What they're really testing:**

1. **Do you have a system?** — Or do you panic and discount when buyers push
   back?
2. **Can you diagnose objections?** — Do you understand the difference
   between real objections and smokescreens?
3. **Are you coachable?** — Do you follow a methodology, or do you wing it
   every time?
4. **Can you stay calm under pressure?** — Objections test composure. Do
   you get defensive or stay professional?

**Red flags they're watching for:**

- No framework (just "I listen and respond")
- Defensive or argumentative tone
- Immediately discounting when price comes up
- Can't give a specific example
- Treating objections as rejections

**Green flags they want to see:**

- Clear objection handling framework (LAER, Feel-Felt-Found, or similar)
- Real example with a specific objection and outcome
- Calm, consultative tone
- Understanding that objections signal interest, not rejection

## The Framework: How to Structure Your Answer

Use this three-part structure:

1. **Your objection handling philosophy** — How you think about objections
2. **Your framework** — The step-by-step process you follow
3. **A real example** — A specific story using STAR method

## Part 1: Your Objection Handling Philosophy

Start with a 15-20 second statement about how you view objections.

**Example:**

"I see objections as a sign of interest. If a buyer is objecting, it means
they're engaged and considering the purchase — they're just not convinced
yet. My job is to understand what's behind the objection and address it
with evidence, not defensiveness."

**Why it works:** Shows you're not afraid of objections and you understand
they're part of the sales process.

## Part 2: Your Objection Handling Framework

Describe the step-by-step process you follow. Here are three common
frameworks you can use:

### Option 1: LAER Framework

**L**isten → **A**cknowledge → **E**xplore → **R**espond

1. **Listen:** Let the buyer finish. Don't interrupt.
2. **Acknowledge:** Validate their concern. "I hear you — pricing is
   important."
3. **Explore:** Ask questions to understand the root cause. "What are you
   comparing us to?"
4. **Respond:** Address the objection with evidence (case studies, ROI,
   testimonials).

### Option 2: Feel-Felt-Found Framework

1. **Feel:** "I understand how you feel."
2. **Felt:** "Other customers felt the same way."
3. **Found:** "What they found was..."

**Example:** "I understand how you feel about the price. Other customers
felt the same way at first. What they found was that after 6 months, the
time savings paid for the tool 3x over."

### Option 3: Validate + Question + Reframe

1. **Validate:** Acknowledge the objection as legitimate.
2. **Question:** Ask clarifying questions to get to the real issue.
3. **Reframe:** Reposition the objection as a reason to buy, not a reason
   to walk away.

**Example (price objection):**

- **Validate:** "I totally understand. Budget is always a consideration."
- **Question:** "Can I ask — what's the cost of not solving this problem?"
- **Reframe:** "If staying with your current process costs you $200k per
   year in lost productivity, then $50k to fix it is actually a bargain."

**Pick one framework and stick to it.** Interviewers don't care which one
you use — they care that you have a repeatable process.

## Part 3: A Real Example

Use the STAR method to tell a story about a time you handled an objection
successfully.

**Structure:**

- **Situation:** What was the objection? Who was the buyer?
- **Task:** What was at stake?
- **Action:** How did you handle it? Walk through your framework.
- **Result:** What happened? Did you close the deal?

## Example Answer 1: Price Objection

**Interviewer:** "How do you handle objections?"

**You:**

"I see objections as a sign of engagement. If a buyer is pushing back, it
means they're seriously considering the purchase. My job is to understand
what's behind the objection and address it with evidence.

I use the LAER framework: Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond.

**Listen:** I let the buyer finish without interrupting.  
**Acknowledge:** I validate their concern so they feel heard.  
**Explore:** I ask questions to get to the root cause.  
**Respond:** I address the real objection with data, case studies, or ROI.

**Example:**

**Situation:** Last quarter, I was selling a sales enablement platform to a
VP of Sales at a Series B company. After the demo, he said, "This looks
great, but you're 40% more expensive than [Competitor]. I can't justify the
price difference to my CFO."

**Task:** My goal was to close a $120k annual contract, but I knew I
couldn't just discount. The objection wasn't really about price — it was
about ROI.

**Action:** I used the LAER framework. First, I **listened** — I didn't
interrupt or get defensive. Then I **acknowledged:** "I totally understand.
Budget matters, and you need to justify the investment." Then I **explored**:
"Can I ask — what's the cost of your current process? How much time are your
reps spending on manual tasks that our platform would automate?" He said
they had 20 reps spending 5 hours per week on admin work. That's 100 hours
per week, or about $200k per year in lost productivity at $40/hour blended
cost. Finally, I **responded** with a business case: "If we can save you
$200k per year in productivity and the platform costs $120k, you're netting
$80k in year one. Even if we're 40% more than [Competitor], the ROI is
still higher because our automation features are more advanced."

**Result:** He took the business case to his CFO, and they signed the
$120k contract. The deal closed in 18 days from that conversation, and they
expanded to $180k in year two. That same ROI-first approach helped me close
three more deals that quarter, adding $400k to my closed-won."

**Why it works:**
- Clear framework (LAER)
- Real example with specific objection (price)
- Diagnostic approach (asked about cost of current process)
- Quantified result ($120k closed, expanded to $180k, replicated approach)

## Example Answer 2: Timing Objection

**Interviewer:** "How do you handle objections?"

**You:**

"I think objections are actually opportunities to dig deeper. Most
objections aren't rejections — they're signals that something is unclear or
unresolved.

I use a three-step approach: Validate, Question, Reframe.

**Validate:** Acknowledge the objection as legitimate.  
**Question:** Ask clarifying questions to understand what's really going on.  
**Reframe:** Reposition the objection as a reason to move forward, not delay.

**Example:**

**Situation:** I was selling a CRM to a B2B services company. The founder
was interested, but at the end of the demo, he said, "This is great, but
we're in the middle of a big hiring push. Can we revisit this in Q3?"

**Task:** I needed to close the deal in Q2 to hit my quota. If I let it
slip to Q3, I'd lose momentum and he'd probably forget about it.

**Action:** I used my three-step approach. First, I **validated:** "I
totally understand — you've got a lot on your plate right now." Then I
**questioned:** "Can I ask — what's the hiring push for? Are you bringing
on more sales reps?" He said yes, they were hiring 10 new reps in the next
60 days. Then I **reframed:** "Here's the thing — if you're hiring 10 reps
in the next 60 days, you want them onboarded into a clean CRM from day one.
If you wait until Q3, your new reps will spend 6 months building bad habits
in spreadsheets or an outdated system, and it'll be way harder to migrate
later. Let's get you set up now so your new hires start in the right
system."

**Result:** He paused, thought about it, and said, "You're right. Let's do
it." We closed the deal for $90k that week. Six months later, he told me
the decision to implement before the hiring push saved them 3 months of
rework. I've used the same reframe on timing objections five times since
then with a 60% close rate."

**Why it works:**
- Clear three-step process (Validate, Question, Reframe)
- Real objection (timing)
- Smart reframe (waiting makes the problem worse)
- Quantified result ($90k closed, 60% close rate on similar objections)

## Example Answer 3: Competitor Objection

**Interviewer:** "How do you handle objections?"

**You:**

"Objections don't scare me — they tell me what the buyer is thinking. The
worst thing you can do is get defensive or talk trash about the competitor.
Instead, I see it as a chance to position our differentiation.

I use the LAER framework, and for competitor objections specifically, I
always ask: 'What do you like about them?' before I respond.

**Example:**

**Situation:** I was in a competitive deal for a $200k contract. The VP of
Sales said, "We're also looking at [Competitor X]. They have a feature you
don't have, and they're 20% cheaper."

**Task:** I needed to position our differentiation without badmouthing the
competitor or immediately discounting.

**Action:** I used LAER. First, I **listened** — I let him explain why he
liked Competitor X. Then I **acknowledged:** "They're a good company, and
that feature makes sense for some use cases." Then I **explored:** "Can I
ask — is that feature a must-have for your team, or is it more of a
nice-to-have?" He admitted it was a nice-to-have. Then I **responded** by
refocusing on what mattered most to him: integration with Salesforce and
ease of use for his reps. "Here's what we hear from customers who switch
from [Competitor X]: they love that feature, but their reps struggle with
adoption because the UI is clunky. Our Salesforce integration is native, not
a third-party connector, and our adoption rate is 92% within 30 days. If
your reps don't use the tool, the price doesn't matter."

**Result:** He agreed that adoption was more important than one feature. We
closed the deal at full price ($200k), and 90 days later, their adoption
rate was 89%. They became a reference customer, and I used that case study
to win two more competitive deals that year."

**Why it works:**
- Clear framework (LAER)
- Real competitor objection
- Diagnostic questions before responding
- Refocused on buyer's top priority (adoption > features)
- Strong result (closed at full price, became reference customer)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. No framework

Saying "I just listen and respond" is too vague. Interviewers want to see a
repeatable process.

### 2. Getting defensive

Saying "Our product is way better than theirs" or "That objection doesn't
make sense" kills trust. Stay calm and professional.

### 3. Immediately discounting

If the first thing you do when someone says "too expensive" is offer 20%
off, you're training buyers to object every time. Justify value first,
discount last.

### 4. No real example

Giving a hypothetical answer ("If someone said X, I'd say Y") is weaker
than a real story with a specific objection and outcome.

### 5. Ignoring the objection

Brushing past objections or saying "Don't worry about that" makes buyers
feel unheard. Always acknowledge and explore before responding.

## How to Practice Objection Handling for Interviews

### Step 1: List the most common objections you've faced

Write down 5-7 objections from your sales experience:
- Price objections
- Timing objections ("Not right now")
- Competitor objections
- Feature objections ("You don't have X")
- Authority objections ("I need to talk to my boss")

### Step 2: Pick your framework

Choose one: LAER, Feel-Felt-Found, or Validate-Question-Reframe. Stick to
it for every objection.

### Step 3: Write out 2-3 STAR stories

Pick your best objection handling stories and write them out in STAR format:
- Situation: What was the objection?
- Task: What was at stake?
- Action: How did you handle it?
- Result: What happened?

### Step 4: Rehearse out loud

Record yourself answering the question. Time it (aim for 90-120 seconds).
Does it sound natural? Are the numbers clear?

### Step 5: Be ready for follow-ups

Interviewers often ask: "What's the hardest objection you've faced?" or
"How do you handle price objections specifically?" Have 2-3 examples ready.

## Variations of This Question

Interviewers ask about objection handling in different ways. Same answer
structure, slight tweaks:

- "How do you handle price objections?"
- "Tell me about a time you turned around a skeptical buyer."
- "What's the hardest objection you've faced?"
- "How do you deal with 'we're happy with our current solution'?"
- "How do you respond when a buyer says 'you're too expensive'?"

## What to Do If You're Early-Career

If you don't have a lot of objection handling experience yet, focus on your
approach:

**Example:**

"I haven't handled a ton of complex objections yet since I'm early in my
sales career, but here's the approach I'd take based on what I've learned
from top performers.

I'd use the LAER framework: Listen, Acknowledge, Explore, Respond. The key
is not to get defensive or immediately discount. Instead, I'd ask questions
to understand what's behind the objection. For example, if someone says
'you're too expensive,' I'd ask: 'What are you comparing us to?' and
'What's the cost of not solving this problem?' Then I'd build a business
case showing ROI.

In my SDR role, I've handled simpler objections like 'not interested' or
'bad timing.' When that happens, I usually ask: 'Can I ask what's driving
that?' Often, it's not a hard no — it's that I haven't articulated the
value clearly enough. Once I understand their real concern, I can reframe
the conversation around their pain instead of our product."
